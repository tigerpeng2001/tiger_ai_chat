# Theology Analysis: The "As" of Forgiveness (Matthew 6:12)

This analysis explores the linguistic roots of [Matthew 6:12](https://www.bible.com/bible/114/MAT.6.NKJV?parallel=2692) and the theological perspective that forgiving others is fundamentally an act of releasing oneself from a burden.

## 1. Linguistic Breakdown: Hōs (ως)

In the original Greek script, the word translated as "as" is **ως** *(hōs)*.
- **Grammatical Function:** It is a comparative particle meaning "in the same manner" or "just as."
- **The Wenli Context:** In the [文理和合譯本 馬太福音 6:12](https://www.bible.com/bible/1578/MAT.6.12.%E6%96%87%E7%90%86%E5%92%8C%E5%90%88%E8%AD%AF%E6%9C%AC), this is rendered as *如 (rú)* in the phrase *“如我免人”* (*rú wǒ miǎn rén*), which reinforces the idea of a "like-for-like" pattern.
- **Logical Link:** It functions as a "correlative," linking the way God forgives us to the way we have forgiven others. The grammar suggests a *standard of measure*.
- **Why it Matters:** The "as" is intended to be a **mirror**. It suggests that a heart that refuses to forgive others is technically "closed" and unable to receive the forgiveness God is offering. Our willingness to forgive is evidence that we have understood and accepted God's grace ourselves.

- **"Similar" vs. "Because"**
  - **Similar (Comparative):** This is the literal translation. You are asking God to match the pattern of your own mercy. It implies a standard: "Treat me with the same measure of grace that I extend to my neighbors."
  - **Because (Causal):** While hōs (ως) is rarely translated strictly as "because," the surrounding context in Matthew 6:14-15 gives it a causal flavor. Jesus follows the prayer by saying, "For if you forgive men... your Father will also forgive you." This implies that our forgiveness of others is a condition or a prerequisite for receiving divine forgiveness.
    | Version | Phrasing | Nuance |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **NKJV** | "As we forgive our debtors" | Direct comparison of action. |
    | **NASB 2020** | "As we also have forgiven" | forgiven"	Emphasizes that our forgiveness has *already* happened. |
    | **文理和合譯本** | "如我免人" (rú wǒ miǎn rén) | Uses 如 (rú), which explicitly means "like," "as," or "if"—matching the Greek comparative "as." |
## 2. The Theology of "The Released Burden"

Forgiveness is not merely a favor to the offender, but a necessity for the victim's own spiritual health.
- **Forgiveness as Self-Release:** To forgive is to put down a weight. If bitterness is a burden held by the self, then releasing that bitterness is, in effect, "forgiving" one's own soul from the duty of carrying a grudge.
- **The Mirror Effect:** By practicing the "release" of others (*免人*), we open the internal capacity to receive the "release" of our own debts (*免負*) from God.

## 3. Influential Perspectives

Several theologians share the view that forgiveness is a gift to the self:
- **Lewis B. Smedes:** "To forgive is to set a prisoner free and discover that the prisoner was you."
- **Archbishop Desmond Tutu:** Taught that forgiveness is the "medicine" for the victim's own spirit; bitterness destroys the container it is held in.
- **Dietrich Bonhoeffer:** In [Life Together](https://www.bible.com/users/tigerpeng2001/reading-plans/22357-the-lords-prayer-explained/subscription/1271966947/day/5), he notes that bearing another's sin through forgiveness releases the believer from legalistic slavery.
- **Hannah Arendt:** She saw forgiveness as the only way to break the "automatic" chain of consequences from a past action, allowing the victim to start over as a free agent.

## 4. Comparison: Forgive vs. Forget

Within the context of [The Lord's Prayer](https://www.bible.com/bible/114/MAT.6.NKJV?parallel=2692), these two concepts are distinct:

| Concept | Action | Outcome |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Forgiving** | Active release (*Aphiēmi* - "to send away"). Putting the heavy bag down and walking away. | The "sting" is removed; the memory is no longer a burden. You are free, even if you still remember the weight of the bag. |
| **Forgetting** | Passive bypass or denial. Pretending you aren't carrying a bag. | The weight is ignored but remains; you may "trip" over it later because the wound was never truly released. |

## 5. Synthesis: Grace vs. Quantity

Rather than asking us to match God’s infinite capacity for mercy, the prayer invites us to participate in its nature. 
- **The Source:** We live in the "Already" of God's infinite mercy, but our own ability to forgive is often in the "Not Yet" stage of growth. Consider God’s forgiveness as an infinite ocean and yours as a small stream; a stream doesn't have to be as big as the ocean to be made of the same water.
- **The Posture:** The "As" *hōs(ως)* is not about matching the volume of God’s grace, but aligning with its direction. God’s forgiveness is a gift (grace), not a transaction. If we could give as much as we received, it wouldn't be grace.
- **The Door:** To receive a gift, your hands must be open. If your hands are clenched in a fist (holding onto a grudge), they cannot receive what God is pouring out. Forgiving others is simply the act of opening your hands to receive the peace you're being offered.
- **Self-Compassion:** If you feel you cannot forgive enough, remember that you are also one of the people you are called to forgive. Sometimes the first step in "forgiving as God forgives" is to accept that you are a flawed human who is still learning how to let go.

*Note: This analysis focuses on the reflexive nature of grace—when we extend mercy outward, we clear the space for mercy to dwell within us.*
